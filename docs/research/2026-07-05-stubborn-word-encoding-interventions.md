# Stubborn-Word Encoding Interventions — Evidence Base & Design Principles

**Date:** 2026-07-05
**For:** The "stubborn word" (`moeilijk`) intervention — what to do for a vocabulary word a learner keeps failing across sessions when *more retrieval practice is already ruled out*. Feeds a feature design (mnemonic/association workshop + resurfacing).
**Method:** Local-first, same shape as `2026-06-28-graded-reading-reader-evidence.md`. Starts from what the project already settles (the `isStubborn` signal, the "labor in vain" framing, Paivio dual-coding), then layers a focused web deep-research pass on the encoding-intervention questions the local literature does not yet cover. **Verification note:** the deep-research workflow's search + extraction phases succeeded but its adversarial-verification phase failed wholesale on a model rate-limit (an infra failure, not a research finding). Every load-bearing claim below was therefore **re-verified by hand against the primary source** (exact numbers quoted); claims I could not independently re-fetch this pass are flagged `[extracted, not re-verified]`.

> **Framing for the design phase:** the decision that *drilling stubborn words is the wrong fix* is already locked (`docs/plans/2026-06-12-mastery-ladder-lapse-and-stubborn.md`; the `stubborn` signal ships as advice, not a drill queue). The genuinely-new design surface is **which encoding intervention, authored by whom (learner vs app/AI), shown when, and faded how.** Spend the design budget there. The research below both *confirms* the owner's instinct (learner-created association + visualization, resurfaced later) **and sharpens it** with three non-obvious constraints: the mnemonic must *rescue retrieval, not replace it*; it must be *shown at the answer moment, not the prompt*; and it must *fade*, because for our exact learner population (Dutch L1) the mediated route is measurably slower than the direct one.

**The thesis this licenses:** a stubborn word is stuck because pure retrieval practice on an *arbitrary, low-relatability* form↔meaning pair keeps failing below the success rate at which the testing effect works. The mechanism-matched fix is to **manufacture a semantic mediator** (an association the learner builds), and then **use the existing retrieval loop to practise *through* that mediator** — with the mediator resurfaced at the answer moment and faded as the direct route takes over.

## Part 2 — The encoding-intervention evidence layer (new; 2026-07-05)

### 2.1 The keyword mnemonic: strong but boundary-conditioned — and it can cost fluency

- **Keyword + retrieval practice, combined, is the headline finding.** Zhou et al. (110 Chinese undergraduates, 20 English–Chinese pairs, 2 retrieval rounds; PMC10839596): combining a keyword mnemonic with retrieval practice **massively beat plain retrieval practice and restudy**. Final-test means: restudy 6.46; plain test-test 4.64; **supplied-keyword + test 9.29 (d = 0.79 vs plain retrieval)**; **self-generated-keyword + test 11.39 (d = 1.91 vs plain retrieval)**. **Strength: HIGH (verified).**
- **The mechanism is a direct fit for "stubborn."** The same study measured first-retrieval success: plain test-test **25.4%**, supplied-keyword **42.9%**, self-generated-keyword **59.3%**. The authors' account: *"a stable retrieval practice effect does not exist when retrieval success rates are below 50% without feedback… mnemonic keywords aid the retrieval-practice effect by increasing the initial retrieval success rate."* A stubborn word is *definitionally* an item stuck near-zero first-retrieval success — exactly the regime where a mnemonic converts wasted failures into productive retrievals. **This is the empirical core of the whole design.**
- **Boundary — imageability.** Campos et al. (363 high-schoolers, Latin; PubMed 15114905): the keyword advantage over rote was **confined to high-imageability/high-vividness words — "for low-vividness words, learning method had no significant effect."** Abstract, low-imageability words don't get a keyword boost from imagery alone. **Strength: HIGH (verified).**
- **Boundary — the fluency/retention cost, in *our exact population*.** van Hell & Candia Mahn (1997, *Language Learning*; **Dutch L1** learners of Spanish + English L1 learners of Dutch): among **experienced** language learners **rote rehearsal *outperformed* the keyword method**, and among inexperienced learners keyword and rote recalled equally **but keyword learners had *longer retrieval latencies*** — with open questions about "the quality of the resulting lexical representations." **Strength: HIGH (verified via abstract + secondary).** Read: the mnemonic is a **scaffold**, not a permanent representation. Mediated retrieval (cue → keyword → target) is inherently a step slower than direct retrieval; the endpoint we want is the *direct* route. This is the empirical warrant for **fading** the mnemonic (2.3) rather than showing it forever, and for **not forcing** it on learners/words that don't need it.
- **The long-delay controversy (flagged, not re-verified this pass).** A recurring claim (associated with Wang, Thomas & Ouellette) is that the keyword method can show *accelerated forgetting* at long retention intervals — the immediate advantage shrinks or reverses. `[extracted framing, not independently re-verified 2026-07-05]`. It is *coherent with* van Hell (a slower, scaffold-like representation) and is **reconciled by the same design move**: treat the mnemonic as a get-it-into-memory scaffold that FSRS then consolidates through spaced *direct* retrieval, and fade the scaffold. Do not treat the keyword as the durable trace.

### 2.2 Learner-generated vs supplied (and AI-generated) associations

- **The generation effect points to self-created — but the margin over *well-chosen* supplied is small and inconsistent.**
  - Self-generated keyword + retrieval was **numerically best** (11.39 vs supplied 9.29) but the difference was **not significant** (p = .179); the authors note college learners "could independently identify effective keywords" (PMC10839596, verified).
  - Campos found the opposite ordering: **peer-generated keywords beat self-generated** (and both beat rote) for high-vividness words — i.e. self-generated did *not* beat a well-chosen supplied keyword (PubMed 15114905, verified).
  - Self-generated *sentences* beat supplied example sentences, d = 0.73 (PMC10873923) `[extracted, not re-verified]`.
  - **Synthesis:** learner-generation is a **defensible default** (generation effect + engagement + zero authoring cost + the app cannot pre-author a personal mnemonic for every word × learner), **but the evidence does not support making it mandatory or supplied-mnemonics inferior.** Supplied/peer/AI cues earn their place precisely where self-generation is weakest: **abstract/low-imageability words, low-imagery learners, and learners who draw a blank.**
- **AI-supplied starters are viable, with one sharp caution.** SMART (Balepur et al., EMNLP 2024): a fine-tuned LLaMA-2 mnemonic generator that **mnemonic experts judged to match GPT-4** at far lower cost, and (arXiv 2409.13952) LLM mnemonics rate comparable to human ones on imageability/coherence/usefulness. **But the same work's central warning:** *"what students think is helpful does not always capture what is truly helpful"* — **expressed preference (ratings) diverged from observed learning outcomes.** **Strength: HIGH (verified).** Design consequence: an "AI-suggested starter" is well-supported, **but do not gate resurfacing on the learner's ★ rating of their own mnemonic** — measure effectiveness by whether the word actually starts being answered correctly (a signal FSRS already produces).

### 2.3 Mediator resurfacing — when and how to show the saved association

- **Pyc & Rawson's mediator-effectiveness hypothesis (Science 2010).** A mediator helps final recall **only if two conditions both hold at test:** the learner can (a) **retrieve the mediator from the cue**, and (b) the mediator **elicits the target.** Empirically, test-restudy practice produced mediators that were both more retrievable and more target-eliciting than restudy — the retrieval group was "more than twice as likely to correctly recall the target." **Strength: HIGH (verified via the citing paper PMC10839596 + secondary).** Two design consequences: **(i)** the mediator is only useful if it is *practised*, not just authored once and filed away; **(ii)** it must be *available at the moment it's needed* — resurfacing it is legitimate and mechanism-aligned.
- **Kornell & Vaughn (2016) two-stage account.** Learning from retrieval has Stage 1 (the retrieval *attempt*, which activates cue-related knowledge) and Stage 2 (processing the answer *when it appears*). Stage 1 potentiates Stage 2. **Strength: HIGH (verified).** This pins the **timing**: resurface the mnemonic on the **answer/feedback screen (Stage 2)**, *after* the learner has attempted retrieval — **never on the prompt** (showing it on the prompt destroys the Stage-1 attempt and turns retrieval into copying). Primarily **after a failure**, which is exactly when Stage-2 re-encoding has the most to potentiate.
- **Fade-out.** Combining 2.1's latency cost (van Hell) with Pyc & Rawson (the endpoint is *unmediated* direct retrieval): the mnemonic should be **auto-surfaced only while the word is stubborn/struggling, then stop auto-appearing once the word stabilises** (still viewable on demand). The app already has the exact stabilisation signal — the `isStubborn`/`consecutiveFailureCount` clears on success; FSRS stability climbs. No new "fade schedule" needs inventing; ride the existing state.

### 2.4 Why words become stubborn — and matching the intervention to the cause

- **"Labor in vain," refined.** Nelson & Leonesio (1988): large increases in self-paced study time on the hardest items yield little/no recall gain `[extracted, canonical; already local]`. **But** Kornell & Vaughn (verified): **unsuccessful retrieval *does* enhance later learning — as long as feedback (the answer) follows.** So the accurate statement is **not** "retrieval is wasted on stubborn words" but **"retrieval *without a richer answer-moment* is wasted on them."** The intervention isn't to stop retrieving; it's to make the Stage-2 answer moment carry a mediator.
- **The relatability nuance (why *these* words resist retrieval).** The failed-retrieval/pretesting benefit **shrinks as the cue–target pair becomes less semantically relatable** — for an arbitrary foreign word↔meaning pair the cue activates little semantic network, so retrieval has nothing to potentiate. `[extracted from Kornell & Vaughn; attribution nuanced — the "unrelated pairs get no benefit" pole is more Grimaldi & Karpicke 2012, while Kornell's own weakly-related-pairs work still shows a benefit; treat as a gradient, not a cliff]`. This is precisely *why a mnemonic works*: **it manufactures the missing semantic relation**, moving the pair from "arbitrary" toward "relatable."
- **Item-difficulty predictors → different fixes.** Distinct causes call for distinct encodings — the design should not assume one intervention:
  - **Low concreteness/imageability** (≈63% higher knowing-probability per concreteness unit; Cambridge BLC `[extracted]`) → keyword-image or **self-reference** (2.5) rather than imagery alone.
  - **Form confusability / interference with a similar known word** → **contrast** the pair (2.5).
  - **Morphological complexity** (an affixed Indonesian word) → **break it down** into root + affix via the existing morphology engine.
  - **Phonological dissimilarity to Dutch** → the keyword's sound-alike anchor is the point.

### 2.5 Other encoding interventions, ranked for this app

1. **Self-reference (learner writes a sentence linking the word to their own life).** Strongest verified L2 result this pass: Hebrew-L1 learners of rare English words recalled **30.3% vs 14.3% immediate (d = 0.71)** and **17.0% vs 10.2% at one week (d = 0.52)** vs translation practice (PMC12426137, verified). It is itself a *learner-generation* technique and needs only a text box. **Top pick alongside the keyword image.**
2. **Keyword mnemonic (sound-alike + vivid image).** 2.1 — powerful when combined with retrieval, boundary-conditioned on imageability and learner experience. Best for concrete, imageable words.
3. **Contrast confusable words.** Dutch-L1 children learning English: contrasted similar words recalled **33% vs 27%**, held at 1 week, via desirable difficulty (61% vs 69% learning-phase accuracy) (Frontiers fpsyg.2021.688160, verified). **Important limit: only *skilled* readers benefited; weaker readers were confused by the contrast** — so offer contrast as an *option for form-confusable words*, not a default. Reuses the app's existing MCQ/distractor infrastructure.
4. **Morphological deconstruction ("break it down").** Little clean effect-size verified this pass, but **strong app-fit**: the morphology engine + Affix Trainer already exist, and affixed Indonesian words are a real stubbornness cause. Natural to *link out* to the Affix Trainer rather than rebuild.
5. **Elaborative / sentence-writing.** Overlaps self-reference; self-generated sentences beat supplied (d = 0.73) `[extracted]`.
6. **Drawing effect (Wammes).** Robust in L1 lab settings; thin L2 evidence and awkward on mobile. **Deprioritise** — a "sketch it" affordance is optional polish, not a core route.
7. **Dual coding / audio** — already present in the app (play button); keep, it's the substrate the keyword image rides on.

## Part 3 — Design principles (the actionable output)

Each tags evidence: **[local]** already-settled, **[web]** new this pass, **[both]** confirmed.

1. **Intervene at the *encoding*, keep the *retrieval*.** The stubborn intervention is not a new drill and not the removal of drilling; it is a mediator that makes the *existing* FSRS retrievals productive by lifting first-retrieval success over the threshold where the testing effect works. **[both — PMC10839596, Kornell & Vaughn]**
2. **Learner-authored by default; AI/supplied as an explicit scaffold, not the path.** Default the workshop to a "make your own" text field (generation effect + the app cannot pre-author personal mnemonics). Offer an **AI-suggested starter on demand** — especially valuable for abstract/low-imageability words and learners who draw a blank — framed as a *starting point to adapt*, preserving some generation. **[web — PMC10839596, Campos, SMART]**
3. **Support more than one association type; let the *cause* suggest the *type*.** One free-text "association" field is the minimum mechanism, but seed it with lightweight prompts matched to why the word is hard: *sound-alike + picture* (keyword), *a sentence about you* (self-reference), *break it into parts* (morphology → link the Affix Trainer), *tell it apart from `<confusable>`* (contrast). Don't build four rigid typed editors; build one note with smart prompts. **[web — self-reference d=0.71, contrast, morphology app-fit]**
4. **Resurface the mnemonic at the answer moment, primarily after a failure — never on the prompt.** The feedback/"Doorgaan" screen (Stage 2) is the correct surface; the prompt is not (it would destroy the Stage-1 retrieval attempt). **[web — Kornell & Vaughn, Pyc & Rawson]**
5. **Fade the scaffold; ride the existing stabilisation signal.** Auto-surface the mnemonic only while the word is stubborn/struggling; once it clears (`consecutiveFailureCount` resets / FSRS stability climbs) stop auto-showing it, leaving it viewable on demand. Mediated retrieval is slower than direct; the goal is the direct route. No bespoke fade schedule — reuse `isStubborn`/mastery state. **[web — van Hell latency cost, Pyc & Rawson endpoint] [local — masteryModel state]**
6. **Measure effectiveness by outcomes, not by the learner's rating of their own mnemonic.** Learners' expressed helpfulness diverges from actual learning gains. If the feature ever ranks/culls mnemonics, use "did the word start being answered correctly after the mnemonic was created," which the review-events already record — not a ★ button. **[web — SMART]**
7. **The mnemonic is precious learner-authored content.** It is a small annotation, not FSRS state, but the learner *wrote* it — so it lives in an owner-only-RLS per-`(learner, word)` table (the `learner_reading_harvest` shape), is covered by backups, and is GDPR-erasable. One row per word; edit/delete allowed. **[local — Operating Context, learner-data rules]**
8. **Don't force it.** Offer the workshop from the existing `StubbornWordsCard` (and optionally a quiet entry point on the feedback screen); never block a session on authoring one, and never surface a mnemonic for words the learner didn't struggle with. The evidence (van Hell: rote can beat keyword for capable learners) says the intervention is targeted relief, not a universal method. **[web]**

## Part 4 — Contested / open points the design phase must decide (not settle here)

1. **Keyword long-delay forgetting** — flagged, not re-verified this pass. Reconciled by the "scaffold that fades, FSRS consolidates the direct route" framing; if the design ever *persists* the mnemonic as the durable representation, revisit.
2. **Self-generated vs supplied margin** — real but small/inconsistent; the design's "learner-default, AI-scaffold" split is robust to either pole, so this need not be litigated. Do **not** claim self-made mnemonics are decisively superior — the evidence won't carry it.
3. **How much to prompt/scaffold the "make your own" field** — a blank box maximises generation but risks the blank-draw; smart prompts and an AI starter reduce friction but dilute generation. Pilot; start prompt-light with an opt-in AI starter.
4. **Whether contrast belongs in v1** — it has the "skilled readers only" limit and needs a confusable-word to contrast against (extra data). Likely defer contrast to a later slice; ship keyword + self-reference + morphology-link first.
5. **AI-starter cost/latency & offline** — an on-demand LLM call per stubborn word is cheap at this scale, but decide the provider path and a graceful "starter unavailable" fallback (the app's honest-failure-copy rule).
6. **Grain of "one mnemonic per word"** — a stubborn *word* can have several stubborn *capabilities* (recognise vs produce). Recommend one mnemonic per **word** (`learning_item`/`source_ref`), surfaced across its capabilities, not one per capability — but confirm against `deriveStubbornWords`' per-cap shape in the data-architect pass.
